Article I

Purpose and owner context

Atlas Core belongs to David. It exists to compound his capital for retirement around 2045. David is an Australian citizen living and earning in Singapore. Tax residency, future residence and estate rules may change; citizenship alone does not settle tax treatment. The portfolio therefore prefers portable, transparent holdings and avoids needless dependence on one country’s tax wrapper.

In plain EnglishThis is long-term retirement money. A temporary fall does not make it short-term money.

The 10% objective is a planning ambition before personal tax and after product costs. It will not arrive smoothly. Some years will be sharply negative. Success means following the mandate through full cycles, not achieving 10% every calendar year.

Article II

Investment beliefs

  • Markets reward patient ownership, but do not promise a return on schedule.
  • A low-cost, broad passive core is the default. SPIVA evidence makes persistent active-manager selection an unattractive foundation.
  • Tilts may improve or damage returns for long periods. They must remain satellites with explicit caps.
  • Risk is permanent loss, forced sale, excessive concentration and abandoning the plan—not ordinary price movement alone.
  • Forecasts and GBM simulations are planning tools. They cannot calculate an “exact” probability for real-world crashes because their assumptions are simplifications.

Article V

Contributions and rebalancing

  1. Keep a separate personal emergency fund; Atlas is not it.
  2. At each contribution, route cash to the furthest underweight eligible holding.
  3. Use distributions and new money before selling.
  4. Do not split a small contribution merely to make the percentages look exact.
  5. Sell for rebalancing only after a hard cap, look-through limit, vehicle failure, tax requirement or changed life need is documented.
  6. Execute liquid ETFs with limit orders during the underlying market’s active hours; avoid the open, close and abnormal spreads.
Article V-A

Whole-share DCA cash bank

Atlas purchases whole shares only. Each monthly contribution is added to Atlas's separate DCA cash bank. Before calculating units, the app reserves the estimated commission and converts the quoted security price into the bank currency using the current planning FX rate. It buys the largest whole number of shares that fits within the available amount.

Worked example

If the bank opens with S$120, a S$3,000 contribution arrives and the approved purchase costs S$155 per share after FX, with S$5 estimated commission, the available amount is S$3,120. The instruction is 20 whole shares costing S$3,100 including commission, and S$20 remains in the bank for next month.

  • Unused money carries forward automatically; it is never discarded.
  • The bank belongs only to Atlas and is not combined with SBR.
  • A recommendation does not debit the bank. Only confirmed contributions, executions, commissions, FX and documented adjustments change it.
  • The bank is cash, not IB01 and not market exposure. It is shown separately in allocation and risk views.
  • Every bank movement has an audit entry and balance-after figure.
Article V-B

Look-through concentration governance

The portfolio must measure what its ETFs own underneath. Duplicate exposure to the same company is combined across IMID, EQAC and SMH. Country is measured as economic exposure, industry uses the provider classification, and asset concentration separates equity, Treasury bills, Bitcoin and uninvested DCA cash.

Article VI

Crash protocol

A fall of 20%, 30% or 50% does not by itself permit selling. Continue contributions if employment, emergency reserves and near-term obligations remain secure. Do not borrow to buy the dip. Do not use leverage, options or margin.

During severe stress, wait at least 72 hours before any discretionary trade. Re-read this constitution, write the proposed action and rule that permits it, and check whether the decision would still be sensible if prices were hidden.

The crash sentenceI knew severe drawdowns were possible when I chose this allocation. The portfolio is behaving painfully, not necessarily incorrectly.
Article VII

Bitcoin sleeve

All direct and listed Bitcoin exposures—including IBIT, GBTC, Grayscale Mini BTC or successor products—count as one sleeve. The target is 5%; there is no mandatory floor and the hard cap is 8%. A halving-cycle narrative, price decline or price rally does not change the target.

Existing underwater positions need not be sold merely to consolidate wrappers. Consolidation may occur when fees, liquidity, custody, tax and estate consequences have been checked. The thesis is considered broken by a durable technical or market-structure failure that prevents Bitcoin from functioning as intended, not ordinary volatility or adverse commentary.

Article VIII

Currency and defensive assets

Income and much future spending are in SGD, while much of the portfolio’s economic exposure is global and many listings trade in USD. SGD is managed against a trade-weighted basket; it is not a fixed USD peg. Trading currency does not remove underlying currency risk.

IB01 may constitute the whole 5% defensive sleeve during accumulation. It provides short-duration US Treasury exposure and operational liquidity, but its SGD value can move with USD/SGD. As retirement spending becomes visible, the amount needed in the next several years must progressively move into assets matched to the expected spending currency.

Article IX

Fund and vehicle rules

Prefer Irish-domiciled UCITS equity ETFs where practical for a Singapore-based non-US investor, subject to personal tax and estate advice. Choose funds by index exposure, domicile, total cost, tracking, liquidity, securities lending policy and operational simplicity—not ticker familiarity.

A replacement is allowed only if it preserves or improves the portfolio role after spreads, taxes, realised gains, FX and transaction costs. No thematic fund may be added without removing or resizing an existing satellite.

Article X

Review calendar

  • Monthly: contribute, record trades, check hard alerts and do nothing else.
  • Quarterly: inspect bands, look-through concentration, data freshness and wrapper fees.
  • Annually: review tax residency, beneficiaries, estate exposure, emergency reserves, fund documents and whether the 2045 objective remains accurate.
  • Event-driven: review only for a material personal change, fund closure/index change, legal/tax change or genuine hard breach.
Article XI

Amending the constitution

No amendment may be made merely because another asset recently performed better, a commentator made a forecast, or the portfolio suffered a normal drawdown. A proposed change must state the problem, evidence, costs, expected benefit, risks and what would disprove the case.

Material allocation changes require a written decision minute and a 30-day cooling-off period, unless a legal or operational emergency makes delay unsafe. Version and date must be updated; the old version remains archived.

Article XII

Continuity, incapacity and records

Maintain an offline inventory of broker, account location, fund names and ISINs, beneficiaries, professional advisers and instructions for accessing records. Do not place passwords or recovery phrases in this constitution. A trusted person should know where the access instructions are stored.

If the owner cannot act, the default is preservation: no speculative additions, no leverage, no panic sale, and no change beyond cash needs, legal duties and restoring hard limits.

Owner’s acknowledgement

I accept that this portfolio can fall severely and remain below a prior high for years. I choose diversification, capped conviction and written rules because my behaviour is more controllable than the market.
