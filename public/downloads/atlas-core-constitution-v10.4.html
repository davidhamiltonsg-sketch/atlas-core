Article III · Contributions

Contribution-first, whole-share regular investing

  1. Add the new contribution and previously unused cash to this portfolio's separate cash bank.
  2. Reserve estimated currency-conversion cost and broker commission.
  3. Exclude any sleeve paused by a confirmed rule or data-quality problem.
  4. Route money to the eligible sleeve furthest below its target.
  5. Buy whole units and reconcile the plan to confirmed IBKR executions.
  6. Carry every unused cent. Never show banked cash as invested.

Worked contribution example

Suppose the cash bank holds S$80 and the new contribution is S$1,000, giving S$1,080. The selected ETF is quoted at US$225, the confirmed conversion rate is S$1.35 per US dollar, and commission is S$5. One unit costs S$303.75 before commission. Three units cost S$911.25 plus S$5 commission, so the confirmed debit is S$916.25 and S$163.75 carries forward.

If IBKR fills only two units, debit only S$612.50 (two units plus the S$5 commission) and carry S$467.50. An execution ID may be recorded once only, so refreshing or retrying cannot debit the bank twice.

Article IV · Rebalancing Policy

Manage drift without constant trading

Portfolio holdings drift from targets due to price changes. Rebalancing restores bands without waiting for new contributions or disrupting compounding through overtrading.

Rebalancing mechanics When a red-zone rebalancing is authorised, sell only enough of the overweight sleeve to return it to the top of its soft band. Direct proceeds through the cash bank and follow Article III (Contributions) to route cash to the most underweight eligible sleeve. A single rebalancing transaction may recover multiple sleeves if cash suffices. Record the drift reason (price movement, contributions paused, etc.) in quarterly review.

Rebalancing worked example

On 2038-Q2, portfolio value is S$500,000. Market gains push VWRA from S$350,000 (70%) to S$382,500 (76.5%). This is amber-zone drift. Route next contributions to EQAC and DBMFE (the most underweight sleeves) and review in Q3. No sale required yet.

By 2038-Q3, VWRA has drifted to S$405,000 (81%) due to continued index strength. No contributions were made due to lower cash flow. This is red-zone drift. Authorise a rebalancing sale: sell S$25,000 of VWRA (to bring it to S$380,000 / 76%). Route the S$25,000 proceeds through cash bank to buy DBMFE (the most underweight at S$35,000 / 7%) to bring it to S$42,500 / 8.5%. Record the reason: "Uncontrolled equity drift due to index performance; contributions insufficient to self-correct."

Rebalancing frequency and thresholds Review rebalancing zones quarterly (Article VI, Quarterly review). Execute red-zone rebalancing at most once per year unless hard caps are breached repeatedly. Green and amber zones do not require sales; they require only modified contribution routing. If a sleeve hits its hard floor due to drift, alert the owner and review fund performance or structure (see Article VI · DBMFE Mandate-Adherence Test).
Article V · Look-through with Quantified Exposures

Govern the risks underneath the labels

"Look-through" means adding the underlying holdings of every fund. If VWRA and EQAC both own the same company, both exposures count. Combine company, country, technology and semiconductor exposure using the latest published holdings. Classify Bitcoin as crypto and DBMFE as managed futures rather than forcing them into equity tables.

Atlas Core targets 2045 (11 years from 2034 baseline). Committing to a phased de-risking schedule now prevents emotional decisions during market crashes near retirement. Do not wait to decide—lock in waypoints today with a 30-day cooling-off period for any changes.

Waypoint lock-in rule These allocations cannot be overridden by market conditions alone (e.g., "wait for a crash" or "equities look cheap"). Amendment to the glide-path schedule requires written evidence, version increment and 30-day cooling-off, except for genuine emergency circumstances (loss of income, major tax change, legal ruling). This constraint prevents emotional de-risking during the first 20% drawdown, when fear is highest.
Contribution routing during de-risk phases During phase transitions, new contributions continue to follow Article III (deficit route) but within the new target bounds. For example, in De-risk 1 (2035–2040), if VWRA is at 68% and DBMFE is at 12%, route contributions to DBMFE until it reaches 15%, not to VWRA.

Glide-path worked example

In Q1 2036 (De-risk 1 phase), portfolio is S$600,000 with EQAC at 5.5% (S$33,000) and DBMFE at 12% (S$72,000). New contribution is S$15,000. De-risk 1 target is EQAC 6% and DBMFE 15%. DBMFE is furthest below target (12% vs. 15% target). Route the S$15,000 to DBMFE. After purchase, DBMFE is S$87,000 / 14.5%, moving toward the 15% target. Do not add to EQAC yet.

In Q2 2037, portfolio has grown to S$700,000 and EQAC has drifted to 7% due to market gains. Contribution is S$20,000. EQAC is now above its De-risk 1 target of 6%. Route contributions to VWRA or DBMFE (whichever is furthest below target). This maintains the de-risk discipline even during market strength.

DBMFE mandate test worked example

Q3 2038 test: SPY falls 18% in the quarter. DBMFE returns –2% in the same quarter. Correlation to SPY is 0.38. Result: Mandate passed ✓ (DBMFE was flat while equities crashed; this is the job).

Q1 2039 test: SPY rises 12%. DBMFE rises 3%. Correlation is 0.42. Result: Normal performance, test passes ✓.

Q2 2039 test: SPY falls 22%. DBMFE falls 12%. Correlation is 0.68. Result: Mandate failed; amber zone. DBMFE lost too much when equities crashed. Initiate 30-day review of fund holdings and recent strategy changes.

Q3 2039 test: SPY falls 16%. DBMFE falls 14%. Correlation is 0.72. Result: Red zone (2nd consecutive quarter of high correlation and poor mandate adherence). Recommend replacement with an alternative managed-futures or defensive fund with demonstrated lower correlation and better crisis performance. Document the replacement decision in Article VI (Governance · Amendment).

Quarterly dashboard template Report the following metrics each quarter:
  • DBMFE daily price change vs. SPY daily change (scatter plot if possible)
  • Rolling 252-day correlation coefficient
  • DBMFE return in quarters when SPY fell >15%
  • DBMFE annualised volatility
  • Fund holdings summary (are they still traded liquid instruments? has strategy drifted?)
  • Comparison metric: correlation target <0.4, down-market mandate pass if DBMFE <–5% during equity crashes

If red-zone criteria are met for 2 consecutive quarters, prepare a fund-replacement analysis comparing DBMFE's cost, liquidity, and mandate to alternative managed-futures or defensive-allocation ETFs (e.g., other iMGP funds, WisdomTree or Dimensional alternatives). The replacement decision must follow Article VI (Governance · Amendment) with 30-day cooling-off and written evidence.

Article VIII · Behaviour and Liquidity

Survive the plan

  • Green: income secure and external SGD reserve funded—continue.
  • Amber: income uncertain or reserve used—pause optional increases and rebuild liquidity.
  • Red: income lost or reserve inadequate—suspend contributions.

No leverage, margin, prediction-led changes or panic sales. Wait 72 hours before an unscheduled discretionary sale unless delay creates legal or operational danger. Personality labels are not investment rules; observable liquidity and behaviour are.

Article IX · Household Governance

Atlas Core and Silicon Brick Road are independent portfolios

Governance choice: Independence (Choice A)

Atlas Core and Silicon Brick Road are separately governed portfolios. Look-through concentration limits (Article V) apply to each portfolio independently, not in aggregate. There is no cross-portfolio rebalancing or offsetting. Each portfolio is treated as a standalone entity for contribution routing, band management, and reporting.

Why independence? Separate portfolios have different targets (Atlas: 2045 retirement; SBR: no end date), different owners (David vs. Dami), and different risk tolerances. Calculating concentration across both introduces complexity without operational benefit. Independent governance preserves clarity and accountability.

Independence worked example

In Q2 2038, Atlas Core's VWRA drifts to 81% (red-zone drift, Article IV). This does NOT constrain Silicon Brick Road's VWRA allocation (which could independently be 72% or 68%, depending on SBR's market moves). If both portfolios independently hit their hard caps, each follows its own rebalancing rules; contributions to one portfolio do not offset drift in the other.

If in the same quarter SBR's VWRA is at 65% (on target) and Atlas's VWRA is at 81%, contributions to SBR route to VWRA (lowest band), while contributions to Atlas route to DBMFE (lowest band after rebalancing). Each portfolio solves its own problem.

If household aggregate governance is chosen in future The alternative (Choice B: Aggregate) would require calculating VWRA, EQAC, SMH and BTC exposures across both portfolios combined. For example, if Atlas's VWRA is 70% and SBR's VWRA is 65%, household equity exposure would be ~145% (70% of Atlas + 65% of SBR). If household risk appetite allows only 140% household equity, rebalancing would route Atlas contributions to DBMFE and SBR contributions to DBMFE simultaneously. This is operationally complex and should only be adopted if there is a household-level balance-sheet constraint or target (e.g., "total household equity should not exceed 65% of total household net worth"). For now, independence (Choice A) is recommended.

Article X · Governance

Reviews and amendments

  • Monthly: reconcile IBKR holdings, cash, executions, dividends, fees, currency conversions and adjustments.
  • Quarterly: review bands, underlying exposures, source freshness, fund status, contribution capacity, rebalancing zones (Article IV), DBMFE mandate adherence (Article VII) and glide-path tracking (Article VI).
  • Annually: review objective, external liquidity, tax/legal circumstances, beneficiaries and vehicles. Recalculate blended exposures (Article V) using latest fund holdings. Review whether glide-path is still appropriate given portfolio size and market conditions.
  • Amendment: written evidence, version increment and 30-day cooling-off except genuine emergencies. Amendments to the glide-path schedule (Article VI) require particular care; do not skip cooling-off due to market timing concerns.

Archive old constitutions as history, but never allow them to remain active runtime instructions.

Plain-English reference

Short glossary

Accumulating (Acc)
The fund reinvests distributions inside the fund instead of paying them as cash.
UCITS
A regulated European fund framework. It does not mean risk-free, and listed Bitcoin products are not UCITS funds.
DCA or regular investing
Investing contributions on a schedule. It spreads entry dates and supports discipline, but may underperform investing a lump sum immediately.
FX
Foreign exchange: converting one currency into another, including its rate and cost.
Satellite
A deliberately smaller holding added around the broad core to tilt risk or return.
Realised correlation
How investments actually moved together over a measured past period. It can change and is not a promise.
Managed futures
A strategy that takes long and short positions across multiple asset classes (equities, bonds, currencies, commodities) based on trend signals. DBMFE is a managed-futures fund, not a hedge or replacement for fixed-income.
Glide-path
A predetermined schedule for reducing investment risk as a target date approaches. Atlas Core's glide-path de-risks over 15 years (2030–2045) to shift from 85% equity to 40% equity and 35% fixed-income.

Worked migration and accounting example

Assume QQQM has a broker cost basis of S$20,000 and is sold for S$24,000 after all sale costs. QQQM records a S$4,000 realised gain and remains in history as a closed legacy instrument. The app does not rename it EQAC and does not move its S$20,000 basis.

When IBKR confirms the S$24,000 as settled cash, it enters the migration bank. The engine then measures every target deficit. It may allocate some cash to VWRA or DBMFE before EQAC; QQQM does not automatically convert one-for-one into EQAC. Any EQAC purchase begins with its own execution price, commission and new cost basis. Portfolio lifetime reporting connects both instruments through the cash ledger without merging their histories.
