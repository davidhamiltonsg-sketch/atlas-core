Article I · Authority

The constitution controls the application

This document is the governing policy for Atlas Core. The cockpit, contribution engine, forecasts, alerts, and reports all implement it. If software and document disagree, stop the affected instruction and correct the software. A dashboard is evidence and guidance, never permission to improvise or override a rule.

Why rules, not feelings?

Market crashes feel like catastrophes. Rallies feel like certainty. Neither is true. A written constitution removes the need to make emotional decisions under stress. When you've already decided your rules in calm moments, a market crisis becomes a checklist instead of a panic.

Targets are planning assumptions, never promises. Regular investing and managed futures do not guarantee profit, crisis protection, or capital preservation. Past performance does not indicate future results. This constitution acknowledges that markets are unpredictable and that the best response is disciplined adherence to a pre-written policy, not reaction to headlines or social media.

Scope of authority

This constitution governs:

  • Which funds and instruments are eligible for purchase
  • How much of the portfolio may be in each fund (soft and hard limits)
  • How to evaluate underlying holdings across fund boundaries (look-through)
  • How contributions are routed to specific funds
  • When reviews are mandatory and what they must cover
  • How to amend the constitution itself

It does NOT govern:

  • When you personally choose to withdraw funds (outside emergencies)
  • Major life changes (career change, inheritance, marriage) that require expert advice
  • Tax strategy or legal structure (consult licensed advisers)
  • Your personal spending or savings rate
Article II · Portfolio Structure & Bands

Approved building blocks and their constraints

Atlas Core holds exactly five approved instruments. Each serves a specific role, and each has three numerical constraints: a target (the ideal long-term share), a soft band (the normal contribution range), and hard limits (floor and cap that trigger required reviews).

Understanding targets, bands, and limits

Target: The intended long-run equilibrium. For VWRA at 70%, this means that after many years of regular contributions and market movements, roughly 70% of the portfolio should be in VWRA. It is not a promise or a current state—just the long-term aim.

Soft band: The normal range for new contributions. If VWRA is at 68% (below its 65–75% soft band), new money goes toward VWRA. If VWRA is at 77% (above the band), new money avoids VWRA until it drifts back down. The soft band creates gentle pressure toward equilibrium without forcing trades.

Hard limits: A floor (minimum) and cap (maximum). Hitting a hard limit does NOT trigger an automatic sale or purchase. Instead, it requires you to stop, investigate, write down what you found, and decide whether to act. This is a pause point, not an order.

How to read a band (visual in Cockpit)

Soft band guidance: A target is the intended long-run share. A soft band guides new contributions. A hard floor or cap requires a documented review but does not place a trade. Identity rule: matching funds correctly

A ticker is insufficient because different investments can share one. For example, there is a VanEck Semiconductor ETF listed in the US (ticker SMH, CUSIP 81569G505) and a separate VanEck Semiconductor UCITS ETF in Europe (IE00BMC38736). They are different funds with different holdings and tax treatment. The identity rule requires matching:

  • ISIN or CUSIP – the permanent security identifier
  • Legal name – the official fund name registered with regulators
  • Exchange – where it trades (LSE, Euronext, etc.)
  • IBKR contract ID (conid) – the unique identifier in your broker system

Why? Because a "migration" from US SMH to IE00BMC38736 is not a simple ticker swap—it is a taxable sale and a new purchase, each with cost basis, realised gains/losses, and new commissions. The app enforces this precision.

DBMFE is not cash, a hedge, or a guarantee

DBMFE is a managed-futures UCITS fund that may take long and short positions through futures across equity, bond, currency, and commodity markets. It is not a money-market fund, a bond fund, or a guaranteed hedge against stock losses. In some markets, it may lose alongside equities. In others, it may be uncorrelated or even positive. Its value is in diversification of return sources, not guaranteed protection. Do not treat it as emergency cash.

Article III · Contributions and Cash Management

Contribution-first, whole-share regular investing

Contributions are the engine of portfolio growth. Unlike trying to time markets or predict crashes, a discipline of regular contributions lets you participate in both bull and bear markets without emotion. This article governs how new money enters the portfolio.

The six-step contribution process

  1. Add to cash bank: The new contribution and any previously unused cash combine in this portfolio's separate cash bank (segregated from personal spending money).
  2. Reserve for costs: Subtract estimated currency-conversion cost and broker commission before calculating how many shares you can buy.
  3. Exclude paused sleeves: Any fund currently paused by a confirmed rule violation or data-quality problem is ineligible to receive new money.
  4. Route to largest deficit: Money goes to the eligible fund furthest below its target allocation.
  5. Whole shares only: Buy only whole units, reconcile against confirmed IBKR executions, and never show fractional holdings as invested.
  6. Carry forward unused cash: Every unused cent stays in the cash bank, never vanishes. This prevents "rounding away" small amounts.

Why contribution-first?

A contribution-first system removes the need to time or predict markets. Instead of asking "Is now a good time to buy?", the rule is simple: "Add new money to the fund that is furthest below target." This ensures that:

  • You buy more shares when prices are low (target is further away)
  • You buy fewer shares when prices are high (target is closer)
  • You never sell just to rebalance (which triggers taxes and costs)
  • You avoid the paralysis of trying to guess market direction

Whole shares and currency cost

You can only buy whole units. If your cash bank has S$1,000 and VWRA costs S$300 per share (after currency conversion and commission), you can buy 3 shares for S$900 and must carry forward S$100. This prevents "fractional share tricks" that obscure true holdings. IBKR tracks the exact cost basis and execution ID for every purchase.

Worked contribution example

Example: S$1,000 contribution with multi-fund routing

Starting state:

  • Cash bank: S$80 (carried forward from last month)
  • New contribution: S$1,000
  • Total available: S$1,080
  • Estimated commission: S$5 per execution
  • FX rate: S$1.35 per US dollar

Current holdings and targets:

  • VWRA: S$70,000 portfolio value, 70% target → S$49,000 target amount → at target
  • EQAC: S$8,000 portfolio value, 10% target → S$7,000 target amount → S$1,000 below target
  • SMH: S$3,000 portfolio value, 5% target → S$3,500 target amount → S$500 below target
  • BTC: S$4,000 portfolio value, 5% target → S$3,500 target amount → at target
  • DBMFE: S$8,000 portfolio value, 10% target → S$7,000 target amount → S$1,000 above target

Largest deficit: EQAC at S$1,000 below target.

EQAC quote: US$225 per share. In SGD: S$225 × 1.35 = S$303.75 per share before commission.

Three shares cost: S$303.75 × 3 = S$911.25, plus S$5 commission = S$916.25. This is within the S$1,080 cash bank.

Confirmed IBKR execution: Only 2 shares filled (market conditions). Debit: S$607.50 (two units) + S$5 commission = S$612.50.

Reconciliation:

  • Debit confirmed: S$612.50
  • Remaining in cash bank: S$1,080 - S$612.50 = S$467.50 (carried forward)
  • EQAC position: +2 shares, new cost basis recorded with execution ID
  • Next contribution will re-evaluate deficits and may route to SMH or another fund

Note: The app records the execution ID exactly once. Refreshing or retrying the contribution cannot double-debit the cash bank.

What if a contribution is very small?

If your contribution is S$100 and transaction costs are S$5-10, the effective amount invested might be tiny. The app carries forward the full amount until a subsequent contribution makes purchase viable. This prevents "dribbling" money into fractional shares and ensures each purchase has meaningful size and recorded cost basis.

Article IV · Look-Through and Underlying Concentration

Govern the risks underneath the labels

"Look-through" means ignoring fund boundaries and adding up the underlying holdings of every fund. If VWRA and EQAC both own Apple, both positions count toward your total Apple exposure. This reveals hidden concentrations that labels alone would miss.

Why look-through matters

At the fund level, you might think your portfolio is diversified: 70% global equity, 10% Nasdaq growth, 5% semiconductors, etc. But underlying those funds are individual companies. Both VWRA and EQAC own Microsoft, Apple, Nvidia, and ASML. VWRA, EQAC, and SMH all own semiconductor manufacturers. Ignoring these overlaps creates invisible concentration risk.

Look-through converts "I own 5 funds" into "I own 1,500+ individual securities with these overlap patterns." This is the reality you are taking risk on, not the fund labels.

The five look-through measures

Measure Watch level Hard review level Response
Single company 7% 9% Pause overlapping satellites; refresh data and recalculate.
Information technology 45% 50% Route cash away from deliberate overlap (avoid EQAC and SMH additions).
Semiconductors 25% 30% Pause SMH and overlapping growth additions; consider DBMFE or VWRA focus.
United States 70% 75% Review whether US exposure is index-driven (expected) or concentrated in deliberate overlays (EQAC, SMH).
Bitcoin 7% 8% Pause new Bitcoin additions; do not automatically sell.
Unclassified holdings 1% 3% Block concentration-led trades (contributions routed by deficit) until classification is resolved.
Oldest required data source 35 days 75 days Mark data as stale and disable concentration-led trades.

Understanding the response levels

Watch level: An early warning. The Cockpit highlights it in yellow, but no action is required. It signals that you are approaching a boundary and should be aware of it.

Hard review level: A pause point. When you hit this level, stop new contributions to overlapping funds, write down what you observe, investigate why the concentration happened, and decide whether to act. For example, if semiconductors hit 30% because prices soared, you might simply carry forward to your next review. If EQAC purchases pushed you there, you might pause EQAC and SMH additions.

Key clarification: Neither a watch nor a hard review creates an automatic order to sell. Estimated or stale data can restrict a trade but cannot compel a sale. You always decide after investigation.

Data freshness and staleness

Look-through calculations depend on published fund holdings, typically updated quarterly. If VWRA's latest holdings are 75 days old, the app marks that data as stale. When data is stale, concentration-led rules (routing money to largest deficit) are disabled until fresh data is available. This prevents decisions based on outdated information.

In your Cockpit, a "Data from 60 days ago" warning means holdings were last published 60 days ago. If you see "Data > 75 days old," you will not be able to execute concentration-led contributions until you refresh the holdings (usually by clicking "Refresh holdings" in the app).

Worked look-through example

Example: Semiconductor concentration discovery

Your portfolio holds S$100,000 across five funds. You check look-through and discover:

  • TSMC: S$4,500 (4.5%)
  • Intel: S$2,000 (2%)
  • ASML: S$2,500 (2.5%)
  • Samsung: S$2,000 (2%)
  • Other semiconductors: S$1,000 (1%)
  • Total semiconductors: 12%

You are well below the 25% watch level, so no action is required. But you now understand that roughly 1 in 8 SGD is in semiconductor companies. If a semiconductor cycle turn happens, you know the impact.

Later, a market rally pushes semiconductors to S$26,500 (26.5%). You hit the hard review level (25% watch, 30% hard cap). You stop and investigate:

  • Did prices rise? (Yes, a chip shortage rally boosted TSMC and others.)
  • Did new contributions go to SMH? (No, you routed to VWRA and DBMFE.)
  • Is the concentration dangerous? (Probably not—it's market-driven, not concentrated in one company.)

Decision: Carry forward. On the next contribution, preferentially route money away from EQAC and SMH (the overlapping growth funds) and toward VWRA or DBMFE to dilute the semiconductor weight. Alternatively, if prices drop later and semiconductors fall to 20%, the watch level releases and you resume normal contribution routing.

Article V · Behaviour, Liquidity, and Emotional Discipline

Survive the plan without panic selling

Market crashes are normal. Recessions happen. External emergencies occur. This article governs how personal liquidity and income stability interact with your investment rules. The goal is to ensure you never panic-sell during a downturn because you didn't keep enough liquid reserves.

Three liquidity states

  • Green: Income is secure (employment stable, business profitable) and you maintain an external SGD cash reserve (typically 6–12 months of spending) outside the portfolio. Continue normal contributions.
  • Amber: Income is uncertain (contract work, business volatility, one earner in a two-person household) or your external reserve has been partly used (unexpected medical expense, home repair). Pause optional or discretionary increases and focus on rebuilding the external reserve.
  • Red: Income is lost (involuntary job loss, business failure) or your external reserve is inadequate (depleted below 3 months). Suspend contributions entirely until income is restored and the reserve is rebuilt.

Why external reserve matters

If a market crash coincides with job loss or a health emergency, panic selling becomes seductive: "I need cash, the portfolio is down 40%, I should sell now and wait for recovery." This is the exact moment when selling locks in losses. An external reserve (savings held in a local bank account or money-market fund, not in your investment portfolio) prevents this trap. You cover the emergency without touching investments.

Behaviour rules—absolute prohibitions

  • No leverage or margin: Never borrow to invest. Margin calls force sales at the worst moments.
  • No prediction-led changes: If you think a recession is coming, that is a prediction, not a constitutional rule. Contributions follow the plan; they do not pause based on your market outlook.
  • No panic sales: A market drop is not a reason to sell. A rule violation or data problem is.
  • Wait 72 hours: Before an unscheduled, discretionary sale (one not required by the constitution), wait three days. This gives emotion time to settle. If after 72 hours you still believe the sale is right, execute it.

Exception: Immediate sales are allowed if delay creates legal or operational danger (e.g., a fund is being liquidated by its administrator, regulatory action requires unwinding a position, or a tax law change creates an urgent deadline).

Personality labels are not rules

Words like "conservative" or "aggressive" are labels, not investment rules. They describe mood, not mechanics. This constitution ignores personality and focuses instead on observable facts: your income stability, your liquid reserves, the fund band violations, and the data freshness. Follow the rules regardless of how you feel.

Article VI · Governance, Reviews, and Amendments

Reviews and constitutional changes

A written constitution is only useful if you actually follow it and update it when life changes. This article describes the review schedule and amendment process.

Review schedule

  • Monthly: Reconcile IBKR holdings, cash, executions, dividends, fees, currency conversions, and any adjustments. Verify that the software matches your account. Check that no data integrity issues have emerged. Time: 15–30 minutes.
  • Quarterly (every 3 months): Review bands and underlying exposures. Are you drifting toward the edge of any soft band? Check look-through concentrations: semiconductors, US exposure, single-company risk. Review data source freshness: are holdings more than 60 days old? Check fund status: has any fund closed, merged, or changed strategy? Assess contribution capacity: will you be able to contribute as planned? Time: 1–2 hours.
  • Annually (every 12 months): Big-picture review. Has your investment objective changed (still targeting 2045, or has a major event shifted your timeline)? Check external liquidity: is your reserve still adequate? Review tax and legal circumstances: any change to your residency, citizenship, or tax status? Review beneficiaries and succession planning: if something happened to you, who would manage this portfolio and according to which rules? Time: 2–3 hours.

Amendment process

The constitution can be amended, but deliberately so:

  1. Write the evidence: Document why the amendment is needed. "I think Bitcoin is better now" is not evidence. "My household income rose 30% and my external reserve is now 2 years of spending instead of 1" is evidence. Or: "A new fund launched that outperforms our current choice on every metric."
  2. Draft the amendment: Be specific. "Increase EQAC target to 12%" is clear. "Maybe add more tech exposure" is vague.
  3. Version increment: Change from v10.4 to v10.5 (or a larger jump if the change is major).
  4. 30-day cooling-off: Unless a genuine emergency exists, let the proposed amendment sit for 30 days. Re-read it. Sleep on it. Market gyrations will make it feel urgent; the 30-day rule prevents amending constitutions every time prices move.
  5. Execute: After 30 days, if you still agree, update the document, date it, and the app implements the new version immediately.
  6. Archive old versions: Keep old constitutions as history (they explain past decisions), but never allow them to remain active runtime instructions. The app points to exactly one current constitution.

Genuine emergencies

In rare cases, 30-day cooling-off is waived. Examples:

  • A fund is being liquidated by its administrator (immediate response required).
  • Regulatory action or tax law changes create an urgent deadline.
  • A major change to your life (disability, inheritance, career loss) requires portfolio restructuring.

Even in emergencies, document the reason. Do not use "emergency" as an excuse to panic-trade.

Understanding your portfolio at a glance

Cockpit layout and visual hierarchy

The Cockpit is a dashboard, not an order form. Reading it should answer these questions in 2 minutes:

Article IX · How to Use the App: Contribution Flow

Step-by-step guide to making a contribution

A contribution in Atlas Core is not as simple as "transfer money and buy." The app implements the six-step process from Article III. Here is what you see and do.

Step 1: Enter contribution amount

Contribution input form A modal appears with a text field: "Enter contribution amount (SGD)". You type the amount (e.g., 5000). The form shows the current cash bank balance and calculates how much cash is available for investment after costs.

Example: You have S$200 in the cash bank from last month. You contribute S$5,000. The form shows: "New contribution: S$5,000 | Current cash: S$200 | Total available: S$5,200."

Step 2: Reserve for costs

Automatic cost calculation The app deducts estimated FX cost (typically 0.1–0.2% of the amount being converted) and broker commission (S$5 per execution, up to 3 executions depending on routing). The form shows: "Gross amount: S$5,200 | Estimated costs: S$15 | Net for investment: S$5,185."

These are estimates. Actual costs may vary slightly based on IBKR's latest FX rates and the number of executions needed.

Step 3: Check eligible funds

Eligibility check The app checks each fund against the contribution rules (Article III.3). Funds may be excluded if: (a) already at hard cap, (b) paused by a look-through hard review, or (c) affected by stale data. The form displays which funds are eligible and why any are excluded.

Example:

  • VWRA: Eligible (at 70% target, in soft band)
  • EQAC: Eligible (at 9%, below 10% target)
  • SMH: Excluded — Currently at 9.5% (hard cap is 10%, approaching limit)
  • BTC: Eligible (at 4.8%, below target)
  • DBMFE: Excluded — Semiconductors at 26% (hard review level 30%, concentrated)

Step 4: Route to largest deficit

Automatic deficit calculation and routing The app calculates how far each eligible fund is below its target. Money goes to the furthest-below fund in this order.

Current state example:

  • VWRA: 70% current, 70% target → S$0 deficit (at target)
  • EQAC: 9% current, 10% target → S$1,000 deficit (furthest below)
  • BTC: 4.8% current, 5% target → S$500 deficit

Execution status modal The app shows: "Submitting to IBKR... " then once filled: "Order #123456 filled: 16 EQAC @ S$323.15 avg. | Debit: S$5,170.40 | Cash bank updated: -S$5,170.40 | Carry forward: S$29.60 | Awaiting settlement (T+2)."

What happens next:

  • T+0 (today): Order is submitted and filled (or partially filled if market was illiquid).
  • T+2 (two days): IBKR settles the purchase. The shares move to your account.
  • T+3 (three days): The app reconciles: did the number of shares match the plan? Update cost basis and execution ID.

If only a partial fill occurs (e.g., you ordered 16 shares but only 14 filled), the app adjusts the cash debit. The remaining S$5,170.40 - (actual cost) stays in the cash bank and carries forward to the next contribution.

Troubleshooting a contribution

Q: The app says "Contribution suspended — liquidity is red." What do I do?

A: You must restore your external SGD reserve before the app allows new contributions. Once your reserve is back to 6+ months of spending and income is stable, change your liquidity status to green, and contributions resume.

Q: The app says "SMH and DBMFE are excluded due to concentration." Can I contribute anyway?

A: No. The app enforces Article IV (look-through). If semiconductors are at 26% and the hard review level is 30%, the app will not let you add more to SMH or DBMFE. Wait for the next quarterly refresh when concentrations may have normalized due to price movements, or make an amendment to the constitution if you believe the limits need adjusting.

Q: The contribution went through but now my cash shows negative?

A: This suggests a reconciliation error. Click "Sync with IBKR" to force a refresh of your account balance. If the problem persists, contact your broker to verify the transaction settled correctly.

Handling a hard limit alert

Scenario: EQAC has drifted to 15.5% (hard cap is 15%). The Cockpit shows a red alert: "EQAC at 15.5% (hard cap 15%). Stop contributions and investigate."

Your response:

  1. Click the alert to open the detailed view.
  2. Verify: Did prices rise (EQAC soared, pushing allocation up)? Or did you keep buying (contributions kept going to EQAC)? Or a data refresh recalculated allocations?
  3. Document: Write in the app, "EQAC hard cap hit. Cause: Nasdaq rally (prices up 18% since last review). EQAC purchases paused. Next contribution routes to VWRA."
  4. Decide: Do nothing (prices may fall later), or pause EQAC additions. There is no automatic order; you decide after investigation.
  5. Take action: If you pause EQAC, the app enforces it on the next contribution.

The key: Hitting a hard limit is a stop sign, not a sell signal. Investigate, document, and decide—but do not panic-sell.

Handling a look-through concentration alert

Scenario: Apple exposure has hit 7% (watch level is 7%, hard review is 9%). Yellow alert appears.

Article XIII · Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about the constitution and app

Portfolio strategy questions

Q: What's the difference between a soft band and a hard limit?

A: A soft band is a normal operating range. When a fund is in its soft band, the portfolio is healthy and contributions flow normally. A hard limit is a boundary that triggers investigation. Hitting it does not force a trade but requires you to stop, review, and decide. Think of soft bands as "cruise speed" and hard limits as "yellow traffic lights requiring attention."

In the app: Green circles = soft band. Orange circles = approaching hard limit. Red circles = hard limit exceeded.

Q: Why does EQAC (Nasdaq) have a hard cap at 15%? Why not let it grow larger?

A: Nasdaq (EQAC) is concentrated in US technology. If it grows to 20%+ of your portfolio, you are making a directional bet that US tech will outperform rest-of-world. That is a forecast, not a rule-based portfolio. The 15% hard cap keeps EQAC a satellite (an overlay on top of your global core), not a core position. It prevents the portfolio from drifting into "guess which sector will win" territory.

Q: Bitcoin is speculative. Why include it at all?

A: Bitcoin at 5% is small enough that a 100% loss would hurt but not break the portfolio. Yet it is large enough to matter in a strong Bitcoin bull year (+200% has happened). The asymmetry can pay off over a 20-year horizon. If you believe Bitcoin has zero chance of ever rising significantly again, you should amend the constitution to reduce or remove it. But the constitutional 5% bet says: "I am willing to take a speculative but bounded position in case Bitcoin proves to be an important asset class."

Q: Why is DBMFE required to have a 5% hard floor? Why not let it go to zero?

A: The 5% floor says: "Managed futures are valuable enough to require a permanent position." If you later disagree, you amend the constitution. But forcing a minimum keeps you from panic-selling managed futures in a bad year and forgetting to rebuild them. It enforces discipline.

App and contribution questions

Q: Why did my contribution go only to one fund instead of spreading across all five?

A: The app routes each contribution to the single fund furthest below its target. This is efficient: it fills the biggest gap first, then the next contribution fills the second-biggest gap, and so on. Spreading small contributions across all five funds would leave each in a worse position. By concentration, you reach targets faster.

Q: The app says I can't contribute because "Liquidity is red." What do I do?

A: Your external SGD reserve is depleted or income is lost. The constitution requires you to rebuild the reserve before you can add to investments. This is not a punishment—it is protection. If you contribute now and then face an emergency, you might be forced to sell investments at the worst time. Fix the liquidity problem first (rebuild emergency reserve or restore income), update your status to green, and contributions resume.

Q: Can I contribute a fractional amount, like S$2,500.50?

A: Yes, you can contribute any amount. But you can only buy whole shares. If your contribution is S$2,500.50 and costs are S$10, you have S$2,490.50 for investment. At S$280/share, you can buy 8 whole shares (S$2,240), carrying forward S$250.50. This unpredictability is why we accumulate small balances and make contributions large enough to buy meaningful share quantities.

Q: The app says "holdings data is stale." Do I have to stop contributing?

A: If data is 35–75 days old, contributions continue, but concentration-led trading (pausing funds due to look-through alerts) is disabled. If data is > 75 days old, no concentration-led trades are allowed. You should refresh holdings (usually 24 hours via fund websites). Once refreshed, contributions resume full functionality.

Market and emotion questions

Q: The market crashed 30%. Shouldn't I sell and wait for recovery?

A: No. The constitution has no rule for market-timing. Selling because prices fell is panic selling, not a constitutional response. If (a) a fund exceeds its hard limit, or (b) an external emergency forces you to raise cash, then you sell. Otherwise, you continue contributions and let dollar-cost averaging (regular buying at all prices) do its work. Historically, investors who sold during crashes regretted it when recovery came.

Q: I read an article saying Nasdaq is overvalued. Should I reduce EQAC?

A: No. That is a prediction. The constitution ignores predictions. If Nasdaq does become overvalued, prices will fall, EQAC allocation will drop (due to price movement), and the soft band will naturally push new contributions away from it. No need to sell or forecast. If you believe the long-term fundamentals have shifted, that is a reason to amend the constitution—but only after the 30-day cooling-off period to prevent impulsive changes.

Q: How do I know if my portfolio is healthy?

A: Open the Cockpit. If you see:

  • All five funds in green (soft bands)
  • Liquidity status green
  • No hard-limit alerts or red concentration warnings
  • Data fresh (< 35 days old)
  • No unreconciled transactions

Then your portfolio is healthy. If you see orange or red, investigate and document. Healthy portfolios need 5 minutes on the Cockpit monthly; troubled ones need active review.

Governance and amendment questions

Q: How do I override or appeal an alert?

A: Hard alerts (red) cannot be ignored; you must investigate and document. Soft alerts (yellow) are informational; you can acknowledge them. The app does not have an "override" button. If you believe a rule is wrong, propose an amendment. Otherwise, follow the constitution. This is the discipline that makes the system work.

Q: Can I make an amendment immediately without waiting 30 days?

A: Only in genuine emergencies: fund closure, regulatory change with a deadline, or major life upheaval (disability, loss of income). Check the "Genuine emergency" box, document the reason, and the amendment becomes active immediately. But do not use "emergency" as an excuse to re-weight the portfolio every time markets move.

Q: What if I want to withdraw money from the portfolio?

A: The constitution does not prevent withdrawals. It says: if you need cash, (1) use your external reserve first, (2) if the reserve is exhausted, then withdraw from the portfolio. The app tracks which positions to liquidate based on tax efficiency and rebalancing. Withdrawals are separate from the constitution's main purpose (growing wealth through contributions). Document any withdrawal in Mission Control.

Q: Can the constitution change?

A: Yes, intentionally. Use the amendment process: (1) draft written reason, (2) propose change, (3) wait 30 days, (4) finalize. This prevents impulsive changes driven by market noise. Old versions are archived as history.

Technical and integration questions

Q: How often does the app sync with IBKR?

A: Automatically every 2 hours during market hours. You can manually refresh by clicking "Sync now." Always sync before making a contribution to ensure data is current.

Q: What if the app disagrees with my IBKR account?

A: Reconcile monthly. Open Mission Control > Monthly Review > "Sync IBKR account." If numbers still disagree, contact your broker to verify. The app is evidence and guidance, not the source of truth; IBKR account statements are the canonical record.

Q: Can I use the app on mobile?

A: Yes. The app is responsive and works on iPhone, iPad, and Android. The mobile view prioritizes Cockpit and quick contribution entry. Detailed Portfolio and Mission Control are better on desktop but accessible on mobile.

Article XIV · Expanded Glossary

Technical and investment terms explained in plain English

Accumulating (Acc)
A fund structure in which dividends or interest are reinvested inside the fund rather than paid out as cash. All five Atlas Core approved funds are accumulating, meaning their value includes both capital appreciation and reinvested distributions. If you buy 100 shares of VWRA today, in 10 years you will still hold 100 shares, but they will be worth more because dividends were reinvested.
UCITS
Undertakings for Collective Investment in Transferable Securities. A European regulatory framework for investment funds that allows them to be sold across EU member states. UCITS funds have limits on leverage, concentration, and derivatives use. It does not mean risk-free (you can lose money in UCITS), and it does not mean guaranteed returns. Bitcoin products are not UCITS even if listed on European exchanges.
DCA (Dollar-Cost Averaging)
Regular investing of a fixed amount on a fixed schedule (monthly, quarterly). Instead of investing S$100,000 all at once, you invest S$5,000 every month for 20 months. DCA averages your entry prices—you buy more shares when prices are low, fewer when prices are high. It does not guarantee profit (you could have bought all at the lowest price), but it removes the pressure to time the market and supports discipline.
FX (Foreign Exchange)
The conversion of one currency into another. Most Atlas funds are priced in USD; you contribute in SGD. FX cost (typically 0.1–0.2%) is deducted when converting SGD to USD for purchases and USD back to SGD for sales. The app calculates and reserves for FX costs before investment.
Satellite
A deliberately smaller holding added around the broad core to tilt return or risk. EQAC, SMH, and BTC are satellites around the VWRA core. A satellite is smaller than the core and serves a specific purpose (Nasdaq growth tilt, semiconductor bet, crypto exposure) rather than general diversification.
Realised correlation
How two investments actually moved together over a measured past period. If equities fell 30% and DBMFE rose 2%, their realised correlation is negative (they moved opposite). Correlation can change; past correlation does not predict future correlation. The app tracks realised correlation but does not use it to make investment decisions.
Soft band
A normal operating range for a fund allocation. Within the soft band, the portfolio is healthy and contributions flow according to deficit routing. VWRA's soft band is 65–75% (target 70%).
Hard limit (floor and cap)
A boundary that triggers a required review. Hitting a hard floor (minimum) or cap (maximum) requires investigation and documentation but does not force a trade. VWRA's hard limits are 0% floor and 80% cap.
Look-through
Analyzing the underlying holdings of every fund and combining them to measure total portfolio concentration. If VWRA owns 2% Apple and EQAC owns 3% Apple, your total Apple exposure is 5%. Look-through prevents hidden concentrations from building up.
Watch level
An early warning threshold for look-through concentrations. Hitting watch level (yellow alert) means you are approaching a boundary; no action required, but monitoring recommended. Single-company watch level is 7%.
Hard review level
A concentration boundary that requires investigation and documentation. Hitting this level (red alert) does not force a sale but requires you to understand why and plan a response. Single-company hard review level is 9%.
Conid (Contract ID)
A unique identifier assigned by IBKR to every tradeable security. The app uses conid (not just ticker or ISIN) to ensure you are holding the exact security specified in the constitution (e.g., VWRA UCITS from Dublin, not a similar-sounding US fund).
ISIN (International Securities Identification Number)
A unique 12-character code assigned to every security. VWRA UCITS is IE00BK5BQT80. ISIN is immutable and permanent; tickers can change.
CUSIP
Committee on Uniform Securities Identification Procedures; a 9-character identifier used in North America for securities. IBIT (iShares Bitcoin Trust) has CUSIP 46438F101. Used for matching US-listed securities.
Reconciliation
Comparing app records to IBKR account statements and correcting discrepancies. Monthly reconciliation ensures the app's view of your holdings matches your actual account.
Drawdown
A temporary peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value during a downturn. A 30% drawdown means the portfolio fell 30% from its highest point (even if it later recovered). Historically, markets have experienced 20%+ drawdowns multiple times per decade.
Cost basis
The original price you paid for an investment, including fees and commissions. If you buy 10 shares at S$280 with S$5 commission, your cost basis is S$2,805 (10 × S$280.50). Cost basis is used to calculate capital gains when you sell.
Capital gain / loss
The difference between the price you paid (cost basis) and the current value (or sale price). If you bought at S$280 and the current price is S$305, you have a S$25/share unrealised gain. If you sell at S$305, the gain becomes realised.
Liquidity
The ability to quickly convert an investment into cash. Stocks and ETFs are highly liquid; you can sell them in seconds. Bitcoin is liquid on exchanges. Illiquid assets are hard to sell quickly without significant discount. The "liquidity status" (green/amber/red) refers to your external cash reserve, not market liquidity.
Cockpit
The main dashboard of the Atlas app. It shows portfolio value, allocations, band status, liquidity, and active alerts. The place to check your portfolio's health in 2 minutes.
Mission Control
The governance and review center of the app. It provides checklists for monthly, quarterly, and annual reviews, and manages constitutional amendments.
Margin
Borrowing from your broker to invest. A margin call forces you to sell during downturns to cover the loan. The constitution prohibits margin; all investments must be from personal capital.
Amendment
A formal change to the constitution. Amendments require written reason, 30-day cooling-off (unless emergency), and version increment (v10.4 to v10.5). Old versions are archived.
Equity
Ownership in a company via stock. When you own VWRA, you indirectly own tiny pieces of thousands of companies.
Bond / Fixed Income
A loan to a company or government. Bondholders receive fixed interest payments. Atlas Core does not include bonds as a dedicated sleeve; DBMFE (managed futures) may take bond futures positions.
Cryptocurrency
Digital money (e.g., Bitcoin) based on cryptographic proof rather than central-bank backing. Bitcoin is the largest cryptocurrency by market value. It is speculative and volatile but uncorrelated with traditional assets.
