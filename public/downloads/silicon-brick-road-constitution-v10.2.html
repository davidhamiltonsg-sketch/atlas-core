Quick Start · Read This First If You're New to Investing

Welcome to Silicon Brick Road

If you are new to investing, this section is for you. Skip the jargon. Here is what Silicon Brick Road (SBR) actually is:

What SBR is, in one sentence

Silicon Brick Road is a simple rule-based investment portfolio that automatically distributes your money across five global funds and then gets out of your way.

The five funds, explained simply

  • VWRA (65%): The global core. Think of it as "buying the whole world." It holds stocks in 3,500+ companies across every country. Boring, diversified, and the backbone of the portfolio.
  • EQAC (15%): A bet on big US tech companies. Same companies as VWRA (Microsoft, Apple, etc.) but in higher concentration. If US tech does well, this outperforms the global core.
  • SMH (5%): A smaller bet on semiconductor companies. Again, some overlap with the other funds, but concentrated in the companies that make computer chips.
  • BTC (5%): Bitcoin via IBIT. Speculative, volatile, but uncorrelated with stocks. Small enough that it cannot break the portfolio if it crashes, large enough to matter if it soars.
  • DBMFE (10%): A managed-futures fund that moves differently than stocks. When stocks fall, it may rise. When stocks rally, it may lag. It is insurance-like but not a guarantee.

How much do you start with?

Any amount. S$1,000, S$5,000, S$50,000. The portfolio will work at any size. Small starting amounts take longer to grow, but the rules are the same.

When do you get money back?

No required timeline. The portfolio has no mandatory end date. If you never need the money, you leave it invested. If a real spending need emerges (home purchase, education, early retirement), you create a plan to de-risk toward that date. Until then: contribute regularly, check the app monthly, and let time do the work.

Can you lose money?

Yes. Markets fall. Your portfolio will be worth less at some point. But if you follow the rules (keep contributing, do not panic-sell, rebuild your emergency reserve), history shows you recover and end up ahead. No guarantees, but a strong historical pattern.

What if you get scared?

Normal. Read the section on emotional investing later in this document. The rules are designed to help you act mechanically, not by feeling.

How much time does it take?

Very little. Spending a contribution takes 5 minutes. Monthly check-in is 15 minutes. Quarterly review is 1 hour. Annual review is 2 hours. Total: about 10 hours per year. Less than a Netflix subscription takes time.

Now, read on. The rest of this document explains every detail.

Article I · Authority

The constitution controls the application

This document is the governing policy for Silicon Brick Road. The app (cockpit, contribution engine, alerts, reports) implements it. If the app disagrees with this document, stop and fix the app. A screen is evidence and guidance, never permission to ignore a rule.

Targets are planning assumptions, never promises. Regular investing and managed futures do not guarantee profit, crisis protection, or capital preservation. You can lose money. Past performance is not future performance.

Why a written constitution?

Markets trigger fear. Headlines trigger panic. Friends' success triggers FOMO (fear of missing out). A written constitution removes emotion from decisions. When markets crash and you feel like selling, you re-read your rules, see that selling is not a rule, and do not sell. Discipline beats feeling every time.

Article II · Portfolio Structure & Bands

The five approved investments and their limits

SBR holds exactly five investments. Each has a target (the ideal long-run share), a soft band (normal contribution range), and hard limits (boundaries requiring review).

Understanding targets, soft bands, and hard limits (beginner version)

Target: The long-term aim. For VWRA at 65%, you are aiming for roughly 2/3 of the portfolio in global stocks. It is not a daily target, just a long-term direction.

Soft band: The comfortable range for your portfolio. VWRA's soft band is 60–70%. As long as VWRA is between 60% and 70%, everything is fine. New contributions flow normally.

Hard limits: Red-line boundaries. VWRA's hard limits are 0–75%. If VWRA falls below 0% (impossible) or rises above 75%, the app asks you to investigate. But it does not force a sale. You decide after understanding why.

Why soft bands and hard limits? Soft bands let the portfolio drift a bit due to price changes. If VWRA rises to 72% because prices soared, that is fine. But if VWRA drifts to 76% because you kept buying it, the hard limit stops you and asks: "Why is this one fund now too big? Is this really your plan?" This prevents accidental over-concentration.

Article III · Contributions and How Money Flows

Making contributions simple and automatic

A contribution is money you add to the portfolio. Instead of trying to time the market ("Should I buy now or wait?"), SBR uses a simple rule: add money on a schedule and let the algorithm distribute it.

The contribution process (simple version)

  1. You add money to your cash bank (segregated from your spending money).
  2. The app reserves a tiny bit for costs (currency conversion, broker commission).
  3. The app looks at which fund is furthest below its target and routes money there.
  4. The app buys whole shares and shows you the result.
  5. Any leftover cents carry forward to next time.

Why "furthest below target"?

Imagine your portfolio has drifted to VWRA 68%, EQAC 14%, SMH 6%, BTC 3%, DBMFE 9%. Targets are VWRA 65%, EQAC 15%, SMH 5%, BTC 5%, DBMFE 10%.

Which fund is furthest below target?

  • VWRA: 68% vs. 65% target = 3% above ✗
  • EQAC: 14% vs. 15% target = 1% below
  • SMH: 6% vs. 5% target = 1% above ✗
  • BTC: 3% vs. 5% target = 2% below ✓ (furthest)
  • DBMFE: 9% vs. 10% target = 1% below

BTC is furthest below. Money goes to BTC. This is automatic and requires no thinking. You do not guess "Is Bitcoin about to rise?" You just follow the rule.

The magic of regular contributions at all prices

Dollar-cost averaging: If you contribute S$1,000 every month for 20 months, sometimes you buy high (when prices are up) and sometimes you buy low (when prices are down). On average, you end up buying at the medium price. This beats trying to time the market perfectly.

Example:

You contribute S$1,000 every month to VWRA.

  • Month 1 (January): VWRA at S$280. You buy 3.57 shares.
  • Month 2 (February, market crashes): VWRA at S$250. You buy 4 shares. (You buy more shares at the lower price.)
  • Month 3 (March): VWRA at S$300. You buy 3.33 shares.

Average price you paid: (280 + 250 + 300) ÷ 3 = S$277 per share.

If you had tried to time it and bought all in January at S$280, you would have paid more. If you bought all in February at S$250, you'd be perfect—but you cannot predict that. Regular contributions average out to a fair price.

Worked contribution example: Your first S$3,000

Setup: You are a first-time investor with S$3,000 to invest. You open Silicon Brick Road.

Current portfolio state: Empty. All funds at 0%.

Contribution form: You enter S$3,000.

App calculation:

  • Gross: S$3,000
  • Estimated costs (FX + commission): S$10
  • Net for investment: S$2,990

Fund prices (current):

  • VWRA: US$280 = S$378 (after FX)
  • EQAC: US$225 = S$304
  • SMH: US$185 = S$250
  • BTC: US$62,000 = S$83,700
  • DBMFE: US$45 = S$61

Deficit calculation (all at 0% currently): All are equally below their targets. The app uses a tie-breaker: VWRA (largest target) goes first.

Routing: Money goes to VWRA until soft band is "full", then to EQAC, then to SMH, etc.

Purchases:

  • VWRA: S$1,894 ÷ S$378/share = 5 whole shares (S$1,890, carry S$4)
  • EQAC: S$1,000 ÷ S$304/share = 3 whole shares (S$912, carry S$88 + S$4 = S$92)

Result: After your first contribution:

  • VWRA: 5 shares × S$378 = S$1,890 (63%)
  • EQAC: 3 shares × S$304 = S$912 (30%)
  • Cash bank: S$92 (carried to next contribution)
  • Total invested: S$2,802 (out of S$3,000)

Why not all allocated? Partly because whole shares, partly because the app prioritizes reaching a rough target allocation. On your second contribution, the S$92 + new money routes to SMH, BTC, or DBMFE (the largest deficits at that point).

Key lesson: Contributions build over time. Your first S$3,000 is mostly VWRA because it is the largest target. Future contributions fill in the gaps.

Whole shares only

The app buys whole shares, never fractional. If you have S$500 and a share costs S$380, you buy 1 share (S$380) and carry S$120 forward. This prevents the "fractional share trick" of pretending S$120 is invested when it is not. Every number in SBR is honest.

Article IV · Look-Through and Hidden Concentrations

Understanding your real holdings underneath the labels

"Look-through" means ignoring fund names and checking what they actually hold. VWRA and EQAC both own Apple. So do SMH indirectly (through suppliers). Your "diversified" portfolio actually has more Apple than you think.

Why look-through matters for beginners

You might think: "I own 5 funds, so I am super diversified." But underneath those 5 funds are 1,500+ individual stocks. Some appear in multiple funds. If you do not know your actual holdings, you might have concentration risk you did not intend.

Example: You own VWRA (global), EQAC (Nasdaq), and SMH (semiconductors). All three own TSMC (a semiconductor manufacturer). Your actual TSMC exposure is higher than any individual fund shows. Look-through reveals this.

The five look-through limits

Type of concentration Watch level (yellow warning) Hard review level (red warning) What to do
Single company (e.g., Apple) 7% 9% Monitor, then pause buying overlapping funds
Whole sector (Information Technology) 45% 50% Route next contributions to non-tech funds
Semiconductors subset 25% 30% Pause SMH and EQAC additions
United States (all companies) 70% 75% Review whether this is intentional or accidental
Bitcoin 7% 8% Pause Bitcoin additions; no automatic sale

Yellow warning: Getting close. Monitor it. On your next contribution, route money elsewhere to bring the concentration down.

Red warning: Stop and investigate. Write down why this happened (market rally? Your purchases? Data update?). Decide what to do. There is no automatic rule; you investigate and decide.

Worked look-through example: Semiconductors at the watch level

Setup: Your portfolio value is S$100,000. You check look-through concentrations and find:

  • TSMC: S$3,200 (3.2% of portfolio)
  • Intel: S$1,800 (1.8%)
  • Samsung: S$1,600 (1.6%)
  • ASML: S$2,100 (2.1%)
  • Other semiconductors: S$3,800 (3.8%)
  • Total semiconductors: 12.5%

Watch level is 25%, hard review is 30%. You are at 12.5%, well below the watch level. No action. Continue with normal contributions.

6 months later: A semiconductor shortage occurs. Chip prices soar. Your semiconductor exposure is now S$30,000 (26% of S$115,000 portfolio). You hit the watch level (25%).

App alert: Yellow warning: "Semiconductors at 26% (watch level 25%)."

Your response: You investigate. Did you buy more semiconductors? (No, you routed contributions to VWRA.) Did prices rise? (Yes, TSMC up 35% in 6 months.) This is a price-driven concentration, not a buying mistake.

Decision: On your next S$2,000 contribution, you avoid SMH and EQAC (the semiconductor-heavy funds) and route to VWRA and DBMFE instead. This naturally dilutes semiconductor exposure over time. No forced sale needed.

6 months later (again): Semiconductor shortage ends, prices fall. Your semiconductor exposure drops to S$23,000 (20% of portfolio). Back to normal. The watch level is no longer active.

Key lesson: Look-through is not about preventing concentrations (they happen due to market moves). It is about seeing them and responding consciously.

Article V · Liquidity, Income, and the Emergency Reserve

Survive without panic selling

The biggest mistake investors make is selling during crashes when they needed the cash. This article prevents that trap.

The external SGD reserve: Your financial airbag

What it is: Cash held in a bank account or money-market fund, separate from your investment portfolio. Usually 6–12 months of your spending.

Example: If you spend S$3,000 per month, your reserve is S$18,000–36,000.

Why it matters: If you lose your job or face a medical emergency, this reserve covers it. You do not touch your investments. So when markets crash, you do not panic-sell because you have cash.

Three liquidity states

Green: Income secure (stable job, not worried about layoffs). External reserve is funded (6+ months). Continue contributions. This is the normal state.

Amber: Income uncertain (contract work, one earner in household, business volatility) OR you used part of your reserve (medical expense, home repair). Pause optional contributions. Focus on rebuilding the reserve. Once reserve is back to 6 months and income is stable, you return to green.

Red: Income lost (involuntary layoff, business failure) OR reserve depleted below 3 months. Suspend contributions entirely. Do not add new money to investments. Focus entirely on rebuilding emergency fund.

Why not just keep all money in stocks?

Because when an emergency hits (job loss, medical crisis) combined with a market crash, you feel pressure to sell. You think: "Stocks are down 40%, I need cash now, I have to sell." This locks in losses. An external reserve prevents this trap.

Behavior rules (absolute prohibitions)

  • No margin: Never borrow to invest. If prices fall, a margin call forces you to sell at the worst time.
  • No panic selling: A 30% market drop is not a reason to sell (unless it is causing an emergency that forces liquidation).
  • Wait 72 hours: If you think you should sell for a reason other than emergency or constitution violation, wait 3 days. Emotions fade.
  • No prediction trades: "I think a recession is coming" is a prediction, not a constitutional rule. Follow the plan regardless of your market outlook.

Worked behavior example: Job loss during a crash

Setup: Your portfolio is S$150,000. External reserve: S$30,000 (10 months). Income: stable. You are green.

Month 1: Market crashes 30% overnight. Portfolio is now S$105,000. You feel scared but your income is still secure and your reserve is intact. Response: Do nothing. Continue contributions if you can.

Month 2: You lose your job unexpectedly. The company goes under. Income: lost. You move to red. Contributions suspended. Emergency: you need to find work and may need to draw from the external reserve.

Your response: You use the external reserve to cover living expenses while job-hunting. You do not touch the S$105,000 (now in the portfolio). Even though prices are down, you hold because you have reserves. Two months later, you find a new job. Income restored. You rebuild the external reserve.

Market recovery (months 5–12): As you rebuild the reserve, markets recover. Portfolio climbs from S$105,000 → S$120,000 → S$140,000 → S$160,000. You are grateful you did not panic-sell at S$105,000.

Key lesson: The external reserve is what makes discipline possible. Without it, you would have been forced to sell.

Article VI · Reviews and Constitutional Changes

Keeping your portfolio on track

Review schedule

Monthly (15 minutes): Verify that the app's numbers match your IBKR account. Check that no transactions are stuck or unreconciled. Quick scan: Do I see any red alerts? This is a health check, not a decision point.

Quarterly (1 hour): Deep dive. Are all funds within soft bands? Are look-through concentrations normal? Is data fresh? Do I have enough income and reserve to keep contributing? This is a step back to see the whole picture.

Annually (2 hours): Big-picture review. Has my life changed (job, marriage, major expense)? Are my targets still appropriate? Should I amend any rules? This is when you decide whether to change anything.

Amendment process (making changes to the constitution)

You can change the constitution. But deliberately:

  1. Write the reason: "I think Ethereum is better than Bitcoin" is not a reason. "I got a raise and can contribute more, so I want to increase growth exposure from 20% to 25%" is a reason.
  2. Draft the change: Specific. "Increase EQAC target from 15% to 16%" is clear. "Add more tech" is vague.
  3. 30-day cooling-off: Let the proposed change sit for 30 days. Re-read it. Sleep on it. Market gyrations will make it seem urgent; the 30-day rule prevents panic amending.
  4. Finalize or cancel: After 30 days, you decide. If still convinced, finalize. If not, cancel.
  5. Archive old versions: Keep old constitutions as history, but never let them control current behavior. The app points to one current constitution.

Genuine emergencies: If a fund closes or regulatory action requires immediate response, skip the 30-day wait. But document the emergency. Do not use "emergency" as an excuse to amend constitution every time markets move.

Article IX · How to Use the App: Making a Contribution

Step-by-step contribution walkthrough

Making a contribution takes 5 minutes. Here is what happens.

Step 1: Click "Add contribution"

A form appears asking for the amount in SGD (e.g., S$2,000).

Step 2: App calculates available funds

The app shows:

  • Cash bank balance: S$50 (carried from last time)
  • New contribution: S$2,000
  • Total available: S$2,050
  • Estimated costs: S$12
  • Net for investment: S$2,038

Step 3: App checks eligibility

Which funds can receive money? The app checks:

  • VWRA: Eligible (at 64%, target 65%)
  • EQAC: Eligible (at 14%, target 15%)
  • SMH: Excluded (currently at 6.5%, hard cap 10% but approaching; app pauses to prevent over-concentration)
  • BTC: Eligible (at 3%, target 5%)
  • DBMFE: Eligible (at 9%, target 10%)

Step 4: App routes to largest deficit

Which eligible fund is furthest below target?

  • VWRA: 1% below target
  • EQAC: 1% below target
  • BTC: 2% below target ← Furthest below
  • DBMFE: 1% below target

Money goes to BTC first. The form shows: "Next contribution: S$2,038 to BTC."

Step 5: App shows what you will buy

Execution preview BTC is trading at US$63,500. At S$1.35/USD, that is S$85,725/share. S$2,038 ÷ S$85,725 = 0.024 shares (cannot buy fractional). Cannot buy any whole shares at this price. Amount carries forward to next contribution.

This illustrates a real issue: Bitcoin is expensive, so small contributions cannot buy whole shares. The S$2,038 carries to next month when the accumulated S$2,038 + next contribution might be S$4,000+, which can buy a whole Bitcoin exposure.

Beginner note: This is normal. Some months your contribution buys shares, some months it carries forward. Over time, it evens out. The app is patient.

Alternative: If BTC is too expensive, continue routing

The app tries the next-largest deficit:

  • DBMFE: 1% below (eligible, cheaper)
  • DBMFE price: US$45 = S$60.75/share
  • S$2,038 ÷ S$60.75 = 33.5 shares → 33 whole shares (S$2,004.75, carry S$33.25)

If DBMFE can be bought in quantities, the app routes there instead, carrying forward the S$33.25.

Step 6: Confirm and execute

You review the execution plan. It looks right. You click "Confirm." The app submits to IBKR.

Execution status modal: "Submitting... Order #123456 filled: 33 DBMFE @ S$60.75 | Debit: S$2,004.75 | Cash bank updated: -S$2,004.75 | Carry forward: S$33.25 | Awaiting settlement (T+2)."

T+2 reconciliation

Two days later, IBKR settles the trade. The app reconciles: Did the number of shares match? Did the price match? Update cost basis and execution ID. Done.

Common questions about contributions

Q: Why did my contribution go to only one fund instead of spreading across all five?

A: The app routes to the fund furthest below target for efficiency. Your next contribution will fill the next-biggest gap. Over several months, all funds receive money. Spreading small contributions across five funds is less efficient.

Q: Can I force a contribution to a specific fund?

A: No. The algorithm decides based on deficits. This is the discipline that prevents you from "chasing winners" (buying funds that are up) or "speculating" (trying to time the market). Trust the algorithm.

Q: The app says I cannot contribute because liquidity is red. What do I do?

A: Your external reserve is depleted or income is lost. You must fix this first (rebuild emergency fund or restore income) before the app allows new contributions. This is protection, not punishment.

Your response:

Ignoring false urgency

The app does not send alerts about:

  • Market crashes (normal volatility)
  • Headlines or news events (not your problem)
  • Friends' investment success (FOMO is not a rule)
  • Your gut feeling that "something bad is coming" (predictions are not rules)

Alerts only fire for rule violations: band drifts, concentration thresholds, data staleness, liquidity changes. These are mechanical, not emotional.

Article XIII · The Emotional Investor's Guide

Managing fear, greed, and FOMO

Investing is as much psychology as math. This section helps you navigate the emotional side.

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)

What it is: You see a friend make S$50,000 on a lucky crypto trade or hear about a hot stock everyone is buying. You feel left behind and want to buy it immediately.

Why it is dangerous: FOMO buying happens at the peak (everyone else is buying too). When the hype fades, prices fall. You sell at a loss, cursing yourself.

Constitution's protection: You cannot easily change your portfolio. To add a new investment, you must propose an amendment, wait 30 days, finalize it. By then, the hype has usually faded. This is a feature, not a bug. The 30-day cooling-off period protects you from FOMO.

What to do: If you see a "hot" investment and want to add it, write a formal amendment proposal. Wait 30 days. If you still think it is good in January, finalize it in February. Chances are, the hype is gone and you realize you do not need it.

Panic selling

What it is: Markets fall 30%. You see red numbers on the Cockpit. Your instinct is to sell immediately and "wait for recovery."

Why it is dangerous: You sell at the bottom. Recovery starts two weeks later. You feel regret. Selling at the bottom and buying back at the top is the definition of loss-locking.

Constitution's protection: No rule authorizes panic selling. The constitution only allows selling if (1) a hard limit is hit, (2) an emergency requires cash, or (3) a fund closes. A market crash is none of these. The written rules prevent you from selling.

What to do: When you feel panic, open Mission Control and write: "Market down 30% on [date]. Feeling scared. Response: Do nothing. Contributions resume as planned." Document that you resisted panic. Later, when markets recover and you feel grateful, re-read this note. It reinforces discipline.

Overconfidence bias

What it is: After a great year (portfolio up 25%), you feel like a genius investor. You want to "leverage up" (borrow to invest more) or "try something aggressive."

Why it is dangerous: You are confusing luck (good market years happen) with skill (you did nothing special). Leveraging up at a peak is textbook mistake.

Constitution's protection: No leverage allowed. Period. Your contributions are fixed and mechanical. You cannot borrow or change the system based on a good year.

What to do: Celebrate good years. But do not change the system. Your discipline is why returns are good. Stick with it.

Analysis paralysis

What it is: You keep reading articles, watching YouTube videos, obsessing over allocations. "Should I do 60/40 equities-bonds? 70/30? Or 80/20?" You get stuck and never start investing.

Why it is dangerous: Procrastination. The best investment plan is one you actually execute. A mediocre plan executed consistently beats a perfect plan never started.

Constitution's protection: The allocation is chosen. VWRA 65%, EQAC 15%, SMH 5%, BTC 5%, DBMFE 10%. No decisions. No overthinking. Just invest.

What to do: Stop researching and start. Your first S$1,000 gets you 90% of the learning you need. The rest is boring execution. Boring is good.

Regret aversion

What it is: You avoid decisions to avoid regret. "What if I invest and it crashes? I will regret it." So you stay in cash, regretting that decision too.

Why it is dangerous: Inaction is also a decision. Staying in cash during a bull market has opportunity cost. You guarantee loss of purchasing power (inflation) to avoid the risk of a temporary paper loss.

Constitution's protection: The plan removes the need to decide. You contribute regularly regardless of your feelings. You will sometimes buy high (regret it later), sometimes buy low (feel good). Over 20 years, it averages out. No need to regret any individual decision.

What to do: Commit to the plan. Understand that some months will feel wrong in hindsight. That is normal. Trust the process.

Social proof bias

What it is: Everyone at work is investing in the stock market. Everyone on Reddit says to buy Bitcoin. You feel pressure to do what the crowd does.

Why it is dangerous: Crowds are often wrong at the peak. When everyone is confident, it is usually late in a cycle. When everyone is terrified, it is usually the bottom. Following the crowd locks in losses.

Constitution's protection: You have a plan independent of social mood. You do not follow the crowd; you follow your written rules. When everyone is panicking and selling, you are the person calmly contributing at low prices.

What to do: Be a contrarian in behavior (follow your plan), but not a contrarian in goals (you are not trying to bet against the market). Ignore friends' returns and market hype. Focus on your long-term plan.

Loss aversion

What it is: You are more afraid of losing S$10,000 than excited about gaining S$10,000. Asymmetric emotions. This makes you overly cautious and miss upside.

Why it is dangerous: To get rich, you need to take risks. But loss aversion paralyzes you. You stay in low-return assets (bonds, savings accounts) to avoid the pain of loss, and end up with small returns. Inflation eats your wealth.

Constitution's protection: The allocation (65% VWRA, 15% EQAC, etc.) is balanced between risk and return. It is not hyper-aggressive (all Bitcoin), and it is not overcautious (all cash). It is designed to grow wealth while sleeping (not requiring daily monitoring or stress). You get meaningful returns (7–10% historically) without extreme risk.

What to do: Accept that markets go up and down. A 20% drawdown is normal once per decade. A 50% drawdown is possible but rare. Over 20 years, the odds of positive return are >95%. Focus on the long-term odds, not short-term moves.

A simple rule for emotional moments

When you feel like changing your plan:

  1. Pause. Write down what you are feeling and why.
  2. Ask: "Does this feeling relate to a constitutional rule?" (If no, stop. Your feelings are not rules.)
  3. If it is a rule violation, investigate and document.
  4. If you want to change the constitution, start an amendment. Wait 30 days.
  5. After 30 days, re-read what you wrote. If still convinced, finalize. If not, abandon it.

This process prevents impulse decisions while allowing genuine changes when warranted.

Article XIV · Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions for first-time investors

Q: What is the expected return of this portfolio?

A: Historically, global stock portfolios return 7–10% per year on average, with high variability. Some years +20%, some years -20%, but long-term average ~8–9%. No guarantees. DBMFE and Bitcoin add diversification but not guaranteed return.

Q: Can I lose money?

A: Yes. Markets fall. If you invest S$10,000 and markets crash 40%, your portfolio drops to S$6,000. But if you keep contributing and wait for recovery, history shows you end up ahead. You only lose permanently if you sell during a crash.

Q: How often should I check the app?

A: Monthly 15-minute reconciliation is enough. Do not check daily (daily volatility is noise). Do not check weekly (weekly moves do not matter). Monthly is the right rhythm.

Q: Should I invest a lump sum or start small?

A: Either works. A lump sum of S$50,000 deployed immediately beats S$1,000/month over 50 months if prices stay flat (you miss the time value). But if markets crash next month, starting small and buying at low prices beats a lump sum. DCA (regular small contributions) removes the need to time perfectly.

Q: What if I need the money in 2 years?

A: Tell the app. It creates a spending plan and de-risks automatically, moving S$X to safety 6–12 months before you need it. The rest stays invested to grow. This is better than keeping S$X in cash for 2 years (inflation erodes it).

Q: Why VWRA and not VTI or other global funds?

A: VWRA is a UCITS ETF (European regulation), low-cost, accumulating (reinvests dividends), and tracks the FTSE All-World index (3,500+ companies). VTI is US-listed and accumulates differently. The choice is arbitrary; what matters is low cost, broad diversification, and consistency. VWRA was chosen; it is fine.

Q: Why allow Bitcoin if it is speculative?

A: 5% is small—you can afford to lose it. But 5% is large enough to matter if Bitcoin becomes important. Speculative small bets with bounded downside are rational in a long-term portfolio. Do not like Bitcoin? Propose an amendment to reduce it to 0%.

Q: Can I withdraw money whenever I want?

A: Yes, but the constitution discourages unplanned withdrawals. Use your external reserve for emergencies. If you need to withdraw from investments, you create a spending plan so the system can de-risk gradually. Random withdrawals interrupt the compound growth.

Q: What if I want to add a new fund I like?

A: Propose an amendment. Write why. Wait 30 days. Finalize or abandon it. The cooling-off period prevents reactive changes. The new fund might be great, but it should be good enough to wait 30 days for.

Q: Is this a guarantee of returns?

A: No. Markets can stay flat for 10 years. Crash 50% and never recover. Currencies can collapse. No guarantee. But historically, disciplined investing in diversified portfolios has paid off. The constitution manages risk but does not eliminate it.

Q: What fees do I pay?

A: Broker commission (IBKR, typically S$5 per trade) + fund expense ratios (typically 0.05–0.25% per year). No advisory fees (this is not an adviser managing your money). Total cost: roughly 0.3–0.5% per year. Reasonable.

Q: Can I retire off this portfolio?

A: If you save aggressively for 20–30 years, potentially yes. A portfolio of S$1M returning 7% generates S$70,000/year (if you do not sell the principal). Many people retire off S$500K–S$1M. But it depends on your spending and life expectancy. Plan with a financial adviser, not just investment rules.

Article XV · Glossary for Beginners

Plain-English definitions of investment terms

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
A fund you can buy and sell like a stock. It holds many underlying securities (stocks, bonds, etc.). VWRA is an ETF that holds 3,500+ stocks. You buy it like a stock but get instant diversification.
UCITS
A European regulated fund framework. UCITS funds can be sold across EU countries. They have limits on leverage and concentration to protect investors. It does not mean risk-free; you can still lose money.
Accumulating (Acc)
A fund reinvests dividend payments inside the fund instead of paying cash to you. Over time, your shares grow in value because dividends are compounding. All five SBR funds are accumulating.
Allocation
What percentage of your portfolio is in each fund. If your allocation is 65% VWRA and 15% EQAC, then 65 cents of every dollar is in VWRA.
Soft band
The normal comfortable range for a fund's allocation. VWRA's soft band is 60–70%. As long as you are in the soft band, everything is normal.
Hard limit
A boundary that triggers investigation. Hard limits are the floor (minimum) and cap (maximum). Hitting one does not force a trade; it requires you to stop and investigate.
Deficit
How far a fund is below its target. If VWRA is 60% and the target is 65%, the deficit is 5%. New contributions fill the largest deficit first.
DCA (Dollar-Cost Averaging)
Regular investing of a fixed amount on a schedule (e.g., S$2,000 every month). You buy more shares when prices are low, fewer when prices are high. It averages your entry price and removes the need to time the market.
Look-through
Analyzing the underlying holdings of every fund and combining them to see your true concentration. If VWRA and EQAC both own Apple, look-through counts both.
FX (Foreign Exchange)
The cost and rate of converting one currency into another. Most funds are priced in USD; you contribute in SGD. FX cost is typically 0.1–0.2% and is deducted before investing.
Market volatility
How much prices move up and down. High volatility means big swings. Low volatility means calm. All markets are volatile; it is normal.
Drawdown
A temporary peak-to-trough decline. If your portfolio was worth S$100,000 and falls to S$70,000, the drawdown is 30%. If it later recovers to S$100,000, the drawdown was temporary.
Cost basis
The total price you paid for an investment, including commission. If you buy 10 shares at S$280 + S$5 commission, your cost basis is S$2,805. Used to calculate capital gains when you sell.
Capital gain / loss
The difference between what you paid and current value. Unrealised gain: You still own it (on paper). Realised gain: You sold it (and owe tax).
Emergency reserve (external)
Cash held outside your investment portfolio (in a bank account). Usually 6–12 months of spending. Prevents panic selling during emergencies and market crashes.
Liquidity status
Whether your income and reserves are healthy. Green = secure. Amber = uncertain or reserve used. Red = income lost or reserve depleted. Status determines whether you can contribute.
Contributions
Money you add to the portfolio on a schedule (e.g., monthly, quarterly). New contributions are the engine of portfolio growth.
Reconciliation
Comparing app records to your broker account and fixing any discrepancies. Monthly reconciliation ensures the app's view of your holdings matches reality.
Amendment
A formal change to the constitution. Requires written reason, 30-day cooling-off, and version increment (v10.2 to v10.3). Protects against impulsive changes.
Cockpit
The main dashboard of the app. Shows portfolio value, allocations, status, and active alerts.
Mission Control
The review center of the app. Provides checklists for monthly, quarterly, and annual reviews.
Whole shares
You can only buy complete shares, never fractional. If you have S$500 and a share costs S$380, you buy 1 share and carry S$120 forward.
Margin
Borrowing from your broker to invest. The constitution prohibits this because margin calls force sales at the worst times.
Rebalancing
Adjusting allocations back to target. SBR rebalances via contributions (new money fills largest deficit) and price movements (prices change allocations naturally). No forced selling.
Equity
Ownership in a company via stock. When you own VWRA, you indirectly own tiny pieces of thousands of companies.
Managed futures
Dynamically managed positions in futures contracts across stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities. DBMFE is a managed-futures fund. It can be uncorrelated with equities.
Cryptocurrency
Digital currency (e.g., Bitcoin) based on cryptography. Speculative, volatile, uncorrelated with traditional assets.
